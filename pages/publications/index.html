Repurposing onshore hydrocarbon wells as deep borehole heat exchangers: A comprehensive modelling study for the UK

W. Nibbs

Abstract:

Onshore hydrocarbon wells offer geothermal energy potential. By converting liabilities into sustainable energy assets, well operators benefit from postponed decommissioning costs while geothermal system installation benefits from reduced drilling requirements. The study herein builds upon previous investigations into the theoretical extraction capacity from abandoned wells in the UK using closed-loop coaxial deep borehole heat exchanger (DBHE) systems. Multi-criteria screening analysis identified 48 vertical onshore hydrocarbon wells in the UK with potential suitability for repurposing as DBHEs. Bottom-hole temperature estimates at these sites are used to select ten wells for detailed numerical DBHE modelling in OpenGeoSys. In order of DBHE thermal power extraction potential over a 25-year operational lifetime, the ten candidates were: Kirby Misperton 8, Malton 4, Gainsborough 2, Crosby Warren 1, Wytch Farm F1, Gainsborough 1, Fiskerton Airfield F1, Stainton 1, Corringham 5, and Reepham 1. The candidate well with the highest thermal yield was identified as KM8 ‚Äî an abandoned gas well in the Kirby Misperton gas field in north-east England ‚Äì averaging 198 kWth over a 25-year lifetime with constant flow rate. The modelling is extended to load-following control of KM8 suggesting that the monovalent DBHE system capacity could meet the heat demands of a modelled agricultural greenhouse with 3000 m2 footprint. While DBHE repurposing could turn existing liabilities into green assets, the UK‚Äôs most attractive candidate site can seemingly only provide heating to a moderately-sized greenhouse without the addition of significant surface infrastructure build out such as cascade heat pump systems. This highlights the importance of integrating single-well technology within a multi-valent system complete with thermal energy storage to optimise efficiency and performance. The pre-feasibility study presented herein demonstrates a screening process and modelling approach which can be applied to abandoned onshore wells in jurisdictions outside the UK.

Controls on geothermal heat recovery from a hot sedimentary aquifer in Guardbridge, Scotland: Field measurements, modelling and long term sustainability

A. Comerford

Details:

Geothermal heat from Hot Sedimentary Aquifers represents a promising intermediate (30¬∞C) resource for district heating systems. A key control on the geothermal productivity of these aquifers is the architecture of faults, which can significantly enhance or reduce the natural permeability of these systems. We present the first three-dimensional coupled groundwater flow and heat transport numerical model, combining multiple data from field mapping and fracture surveys, of two intersecting major fault systems in Central-East Scotland. This includes fault-zone fracture permeability modelling, depth-dependent permeability modelling, geo-mechanical facies assessment and heat productivity estimates for single well and multiple well extraction scenarios. Simulations indicate that with careful location of extraction wells within permeable fault systems, production is sustainable for over 50 years for multiple-well extraction scenarios in this region.
